Xray Free Electron Lasers (XFEL)


The xray free electron laser or XFEL is a device well known as a ideal example of the application of accelerator physics and technology to the broader scientific community. They employ the general principle that accelerating charged particles radiate photons in order to generate very bright coherent bunches of xray photons which can be used in fields as diverse as biology to materials engineering. Large scale national level labs are their exclusive terrain as of the writing of this article. The first was the LCLS at SLAC National Lab in Menlo Park, California. Since then several additional XFELs have been successfully built and commissioned.

 

The XFEL is built upon the electron linear accelerator or linac. The US Particle Accelerator School has many useful materials for introducing oneself to the terminology and concepts involved in particle accelerator science. The most recent fundamentals course presentation I would recommend reviewing can be found here. One compelling direction for XFEL development is shrinking the size of the device such that they can be used in smaller scale laboratories, increasing accessibility to a very useful scientific tool. The Ultra-Comptact XFEL is one notable concept for a university scale XFEL.
